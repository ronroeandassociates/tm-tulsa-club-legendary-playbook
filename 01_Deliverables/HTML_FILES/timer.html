Meeting Role Script

Timer

The Timer helps protect meeting quality by keeping speakers, evaluators, and segments within their allotted time. Good timing keeps the meeting orderly, fair, and professional.

Jump to Sample Script Meeting-Day Checklist

Role Purpose

Your job is to track time accurately, signal speakers at the proper thresholds, and report timing results clearly when called upon. Precision matters. You are supporting both fairness and flow.

What You Do

  • Time prepared speeches, evaluations, and Table Topics responses.
  • Show the correct visual signal at the correct time.
  • Record final times accurately.
  • Deliver a concise timing report when asked.

What Good Looks Like

  • You are ready before the meeting begins.
  • Your timing signals are easy to see.
  • Your report is short, clear, and accurate.
  • You help the meeting stay on schedule.

Core Responsibilities

  1. Arrive early enough to confirm that timing equipment or timing cards are available and visible.
  2. Review the agenda so you know which segments need to be timed.
  3. Confirm timing standards with the Presiding Officer, Toastmaster, or General Evaluator if anything is unclear.
  4. Track each speaker and evaluator carefully.
  5. Deliver your report only when called upon.
Timing standards can vary slightly by speech project, contest, or special format. When in doubt, verify the target times before the meeting starts.

Common Timing Standards

These are common club-level examples. Use the actual agenda or instructions for the meeting if different times are assigned.

Table Topics

Green · 1:00

Yellow · 1:30

Red · 2:00

Speech Evaluation

Green · 2:00

Yellow · 2:30

Red · 3:00

Prepared Speech Example

Green · 5:00

Yellow · 6:00

Red · 7:00

Sample Script

Adjust this to fit the meeting agenda and the instructions given by the Toastmaster or Presiding Officer.

Introduction:

“As Timer, I will monitor the time for prepared speeches, evaluations, and Table Topics. I will show the green signal, yellow signal, and red signal at the appropriate times for each segment. When called upon, I will report the time for each speaker.”

Timing explanation example:

“For Table Topics, I will show green at 1 minute, yellow at 1 minute 30 seconds, and red at 2 minutes. For evaluations, I will show green at 2 minutes, yellow at 2 minutes 30 seconds, and red at 3 minutes.”

Timing report example:

“Madeline’s Table Topic was 1 minute 42 seconds. Andrew’s Table Topic was 1 minute 57 seconds. Ron’s evaluation was 2 minutes 41 seconds. Wanda’s evaluation was 2 minutes 28 seconds.”

Meeting-Day Checklist

  • Bring or locate timing cards, lights, or approved timing device.
  • Review the agenda before the meeting starts.
  • Mark the time range for each speaker and evaluator.
  • Make sure your signals are visible from where speakers will stand.
  • Record each time immediately after the speaker finishes.
  • Keep your report brief and accurate.

Quick Tips

Be Visible

Position yourself where speakers can see your timing signals without having to search for them.

Be Neutral

Your role is accuracy, not commentary. Report the times plainly and without adding evaluation.

Be Ready

Have a pen, agenda, and timing device ready before the meeting starts so there is no delay.
