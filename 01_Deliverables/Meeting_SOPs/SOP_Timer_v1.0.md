# Purpose

The Timer monitors and reports the speaking time for meeting participants.

This role helps ensure that:

- speakers practice time discipline
- meeting segments remain balanced
- the meeting stays on schedule

Accurate timing is essential for fair evaluations and effective meeting management.

---

# Core Responsibilities

The Timer is responsible for:

- tracking speaking time during the meeting
- providing timing signals to speakers
- recording each speaker’s time
- delivering the timing report during the evaluation segment

The Timer supports both speakers and meeting leadership.

---

# Preparation

Preparation should begin before the meeting.

Tasks include:

- reviewing the meeting agenda
- understanding timing requirements for each role
- ensuring timing equipment is available

Timing equipment may include:

- timing cards (green, yellow, red)
- stopwatch or timer
- timing report sheet

---

# Arrival

The Timer should arrive **15–20 minutes before the meeting begins**.

Arrival tasks:

- confirm timing equipment is ready
- review the meeting agenda
- coordinate with the Toastmaster and General Evaluator

Early preparation prevents timing errors.

---

# Timing Signals

The Timer uses colored signals to communicate time limits.

Typical signals include:

| Color | Meaning |
|------|--------|
Green | minimum speaking time reached |
Yellow | approaching speaking limit |
Red | maximum speaking time reached |

These signals help speakers manage their delivery.

---

# Table Topics Timing

Standard timing for Table Topics:

| Signal | Time |
|------|------|
Green | 1 minute |
Yellow | 1 minute 30 seconds |
Red | 2 minutes |

Participants should attempt to conclude their remarks within this range.

---

# Prepared Speech Timing

Prepared speeches typically follow project guidelines.

Common timing ranges include:

| Speech Type | Time Range |
|-------------|-----------|
Ice Breaker | 4–6 minutes |
Standard Project | 5–7 minutes |
Advanced Project | 8–10 minutes |

The Timer should confirm speech timing before the meeting begins.

---

# Evaluation Timing

Speech evaluations typically follow this range:

| Signal | Time |
|------|------|
Green | 2 minutes |
Yellow | 2 minutes 30 seconds |
Red | 3 minutes |

Evaluators should provide meaningful feedback within this timeframe.

---

# Recording Times

The Timer should record the actual speaking time for each participant.

Participants to track include:

- Table Topics speakers
- prepared speech speakers
- evaluators

Accurate records are necessary for the timing report.

---

# Timing Report

During the evaluation segment the Timer delivers the timing report.

The report typically includes:

- names of speakers
- recorded speaking times

Example:

“Speaker John Smith: 5 minutes 45 seconds.”

The report should be concise and clear.

---

# Integration with Meeting Flow

The Timer works closely with:

- Toastmaster of the Day
- General Evaluator
- Table Topics Master

Clear coordination ensures smooth meeting pacing.

---

# Time Discipline

Maintaining time discipline benefits the entire meeting.

Benefits include:

- fair speaking opportunities
- consistent meeting duration
- improved speaker preparation

The Timer helps maintain these standards.

---

# Common Failure Points

| Issue | Prevention |
|------|-------------|
Missed timing signals | focus on each speaker |
Incorrect time records | track times carefully |
Signals not visible | hold cards clearly |
Confusion about speech timing | confirm with Toastmaster beforehand |

---

# Success Indicators

The Timer is successful when:

- speakers receive clear timing signals
- meeting segments remain on schedule
- timing reports are accurate
- participants become more aware of speaking time

---

# Continuous Improvement

Members serving as Timer should:

- practice accurate time tracking
- observe experienced timers
- remain attentive during speeches

Developing strong timing skills contributes to overall meeting professionalism.
