Meeting Role Script

Ah-Counter

The Ah-Counter helps members become more aware of filler words, repeated phrases, and distracting speech habits. Done well, this role strengthens listening, supports speaker growth, and improves overall communication quality.

Jump to Sample Script Meeting-Day Checklist

Role Purpose

Your job is to listen carefully throughout the meeting and note verbal fillers or distracting habits that speakers may not realize they are using. The goal is not criticism. The goal is awareness and improvement.

What You Do

  • Listen for filler words such as “ah,” “um,” “so,” or “you know.”
  • Note repeated words or phrases when they become distracting.
  • Track accidental sounds or verbal crutches when appropriate.
  • Report your findings clearly and respectfully when called upon.

What Good Looks Like

  • You listen carefully to the whole meeting.
  • Your notes are accurate and organized.
  • Your report is concise and constructive.
  • You help members notice patterns they can improve.

Core Responsibilities

  1. Arrive ready with a pen, agenda, and note sheet or device for tracking.
  2. Listen throughout the meeting, not only during prepared speeches.
  3. Track filler words and distracting habits in a fair and consistent way.
  4. Keep your report brief, specific, and helpful.
  5. Deliver your report only when called upon by the Toastmaster, General Evaluator, or Presiding Officer.
Not every repeated word needs to be reported. Focus on patterns that are noticeable enough to affect clarity, flow, or polish.

What to Listen For

Track the habits that most commonly weaken clarity or make a speaker sound less polished. Use judgment and stay constructive.

Filler Words

Classic Fillers

Examples include “ah,” “um,” “er,” and “uh.” These are the most common items to count.

Repeated Phrases

Verbal Crutches

Examples include “you know,” “like,” “so,” “I mean,” or “basically” when used repeatedly.

Distracting Sounds

Unintended Noises

Examples can include throat-clearing, repeated nervous laughter, or other sounds that break fluency.

Pattern Awareness

Repeated Words

Notice when a speaker relies heavily on the same transition or phrase over and over in a short time span.

Sample Script

Adjust this script to fit the meeting tone and the instructions given by the Toastmaster or General Evaluator.

Introduction:

“As Ah-Counter, I will listen for filler words, repeated words or phrases, and other distracting speech habits during the meeting. When called upon, I will provide a brief report to help speakers become more aware of these habits and continue improving.”

Report example:

“During today’s meeting, I noted several filler words and repeated phrases. Madeline used ‘um’ twice. Andrew used ‘you know’ three times. Ron used ‘so’ repeatedly during transitions. Overall, the meeting was strong, and these notes are simply to help us sharpen our communication.”

Meeting-Day Checklist

  • Bring a pen, agenda, and note sheet or tracking device.
  • Mark each speaker’s name before the meeting starts if possible.
  • Listen during every major speaking segment.
  • Record patterns, not random isolated moments.
  • Keep your final report short, specific, and respectful.
  • Speak with a constructive tone when delivering your report.

Quick Tips

Be Constructive

Your report should help people improve, not make them self-conscious or defensive.

Be Selective

Focus on noticeable habits. Do not overload the report with minor items that do not materially matter.

Be Consistent

Use the same standard across speakers so your report feels fair and useful.
